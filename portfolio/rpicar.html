Autonomous Robotic Car

Overview

This is a coursework project that we built in a team of 4 as part of our Robotics course at Imperial. It is a Raspberry Pi-operated robotic car that had to perform a series of challenges including localization, autonomous navigation, place recognition, wall following, obstacle detection, etc. There was a series of different challenges and milestones that ran for 3 months. In all of them the robot had to operate autonomously.

PID Control

The first challenge was to calibrate the robot to be extremely precise. We were given a piece of paper and the robot had to draw a perfect square 10 times in a row. We got to try Ziegler-Nichols tuning method and then adjust the values additionally based on our own reasoning and robot behaviour. I finally got to learn how to calibrate PID controller. We even managed to find a bug in the template software implementation of the PID controller that we were given.

Monte Carlo Localization

I enjoyed this challenge a lot as it involved a lot of maths and a fairly complex algorithm. I also learned a lot about probabilistic robotics. There was again a lot of tuning involved but we did very well. It was also fun working with the extremely bad Lego Mindstorm sonar sensor as I had to write a filtering algorithm to infer and reject incorrect data.

Occupancy Mapping

Very enjoyable as maths and probabilistic robotics were involved again. Got to build a map of surroundings based on sonar readings.

Grand Challenge

We were supposed to detect and bump into 3 bottles which were placed on a given arena as fast as possible. Every group had freedom to choose their own approach. We were only allowed to use a rotating sonar sensor. We decided to go for place recognition. First we acquired readings of the sonar sensor at specificly chosen points on the arena (without any bottles) in order to build our places database. At the real challenge we reaquired readings at the same points and compared them to the ones we had in our database to detect anomalies, and hence, the bottles. There were substantially complex algorithms and filtering involved, which was very cool. We achieved extremely good accuracy.
